## Preference Verdict

**Clear winner: Concept B** with 9 of 10 votes (90%). The single dissent came from Shota Kimura (Young Field Rep), who chose Concept A on admin-reduction grounds specific to solo field reporting. Concept C received zero votes. The margin is decisive across every professional context tested — enterprise, SMB, government, startup, and freelance — making this one of the most uniform preference splits possible at this panel size.

---

## Key Findings

1. **The "execution gap" framing resonated universally, collapsing an otherwise diverse panel.** Personas as different as a startup COO (Yuki), a manufacturing GM (Masahiro), a government section chief (Noboru), and a freelance UX designer (Ayaka) all converged on the same diagnosis: the problem is not in-meeting capture, it is what happens after the call ends — decisions forgotten, ownership ambiguous, follow-ups dropped. Concept B's direct address of that failure mode made it the default choice even among skeptics who had no intention of buying imminently.

2. **Concept A was widely acknowledged but dismissed as "table stakes."** Haruka Fujimoto articulated the consensus explicitly — automated transcription and summary are now category-baseline expectations, not differentiators. Even Shota, the sole A-voter, chose it for a use case (solo field rep reporting) that is structurally different from team accountability; his reasoning does not generalize to the other nine segments. Concept A's pitch carries no differentiation signal for anyone managing multi-stakeholder workflows.

3. **Concept C was unanimously associated with sales teams and manager surveillance, creating active rejection.** Every persona who commented on C used language like "pipeline review," "VP of Sales wants this," or "manager visibility," immediately disqualifying it for their own context. Shota's defection from C to A was partly driven by anxiety about increased monitoring. C's framing as a sales-management tool has narrowed its perceived relevance too aggressively for a general-purpose meeting tool positioning.

4. **Purchase intent is suppressed by a compliance firewall, not by concept skepticism.** All five personas who scored purchase likelihood at 2 (Masahiro, Rina, Takeshi, Sachiko, Noboru) were convinced by Concept B's value logic — they simply could not clear an internal security or proof-of-concept hurdle. This means the intent gap is structural and addressable, not attitudinal. The five personas who scored 4 (Kenji, Yuki, Ayaka, Shota, Haruka) are lower-friction buyers concentrated in startup, freelance, DX, and field-rep roles.

5. **Japanese-market-specific requirements surfaced as a hard gating condition in enterprise and SMB contexts.** Japanese-language support (including keigo and code-switching), Japan-region data hosting, and social-proof from comparable Japanese companies were cited as non-negotiable prerequisites, not nice-to-haves, by Haruka, Masahiro, and Sachiko respectively. Without these, enterprise and regulated-sector sales cycles cannot even begin.

## Segment / Profile Splits

All ten segments chose B, but for notably different reasons, and purchase intent splits cleanly by organizational context:

- **High-intent B choosers** (PL=4): Efficiency-Obsessed Manager, Startup Juggler, Remote Creative Freelancer, Innovation Champion — these personas are empowered buyers with low procurement friction; accountability and workflow-integration features drive their enthusiasm.
- **Low-intent B choosers** (PL=2): Analog Traditionalist, Overwhelmed Section Chief, Skeptical IT Gatekeeper, Government Bureaucrat — conceptual alignment is present but security compliance, proof requirements, or Japanese-language gaps block conversion. These are not lost accounts; they are pre-sales requirements problems.
- **Mid-intent B chooser** (PL=3): Cost-Conscious SMB Owner (Sachiko) — she believes in the value case but requires data residency clarity and predictable flat-fee pricing before committing.
- **Sole A voter** (PL=4): Young Field Rep (Shota) — his use case is individual admin reduction after solo client visits, a fundamentally different workflow from team accountability. Note: each segment has N=1; individual-level patterns should not be over-indexed as segment-level rules.

## Purchase-Intent Drivers

**What pushed intent up**: Direct personal experience of the post-meeting follow-up failure (Kenji, Yuki, Sachiko), low internal procurement friction (Ayaka, Shota), and ability to construct a defensible internal ROI narrative around "measurably improved follow-through rate" rather than time savings (Haruka). **What suppressed intent**: Data residency and security certification requirements in financial, enterprise, and public-sector contexts blocked Rina, Takeshi, and Sachiko from even beginning an internal approval process; for Masahiro and Noboru, the absence of Japanese-company social proof and Japanese-language interfaces functioned as equivalent blockers. These are pre-purchase hurdles that must be removed at the top of the funnel before benefit claims reach decision-makers.

## Per-Concept Strengths and Weaknesses

### Concept B — 9/10 chose this

**Strengths (cross-persona):** Concept B's core insight — that meeting failure occurs in the hours and days after the call, not during it — resonated across every professional archetype tested. Enterprise managers (Kenji, Rina), startup operators (Yuki), and public-sector administrators (Noboru) all independently described versions of the same pain: commitments quietly dropped, decisions reinterpreted, and ownership never established. Haruka framed the organizational cost in ROI terms directly applicable to a Japanese enterprise business case. The concept succeeded because it named a failure mode that feels universal and deeply personal.

**Weaknesses (cross-persona):** The concept as presented says nothing about data handling, which proved to be the single largest conversion blocker. Takeshi stated bluntly that none of the three concepts provided information necessary for a procurement decision. Rina could not advance internally without documentation of private-cloud or on-premise deployment options. The concept also lacks built-in ownership mechanics — Kenji specifically flagged the absence of named owners, hard deadlines, and automated status nudges, meaning even committed B supporters see an accountability gap in the execution model itself.

---

### Concept A — 1/10 chose this

**Strengths (cross-persona):** The admin-reduction value proposition is real and understood. Shota's endorsement reflects a genuine segment need — high-frequency, solo-encounter workers (field reps, individual consultants) whose bottleneck is post-meeting report generation rather than team accountability. Ayaka also acknowledged the in-session note-taking burden as a valid pain point before concluding that post-call follow-up mattered more.

**Weaknesses (cross-persona):** Across nine of ten personas, Concept A's pitch was categorized as category-standard rather than differentiated. Haruka called it "table stakes"; Yuki called it "marketing boilerplate." The concept positions on the wrong moment in the workflow for anyone managing multi-person commitments. Without a meaningful differentiator beyond transcription automation, it cannot command a preference vote from experienced buyers.

---

### Concept C — 0/10 chose this

**Strengths (cross-persona):** No persona chose Concept C, and reactions were predominantly negative. The concept was not meaningfully tested as a competitive option within this panel.

**Weaknesses (cross-persona):** Every persona who commented associated C exclusively with sales team surveillance and CRM pipeline management. Yuki dismissed it as a tool "a VP of Sales at a 300-person company wants." Shota reported it made him feel monitored. Rina and Haruka noted it was irrelevant to corporate planning and cross-functional DX deployment respectively. The framing around "manager visibility into customer calls" introduced surveillance connotations that actively harmed reception. If Concept C is intended for a broader audience, its current language disqualifies it before the value proposition is even evaluated.

## Improvement Theme Clusters

- **Data residency & security certification** (5 mentions across Concepts B, A — Rina, Takeshi, Sachiko, Masahiro, Noboru) — Explicit demand for Japan-region hosting, private-cloud/on-premise deployment options, and certifications compatible with financial-sector and public-sector information security standards; without these, internal approval processes cannot start.

- **Structured ownership & accountability mechanics** (3 mentions across Concept B — Kenji, Yuki, Noboru) — Auto-assignment of named owners and hard deadlines to captured action items, automated follow-up nudges for overdue items, and (in Noboru's case) a formal human-review-and-approval step before records are distributed.

- **Japanese-language & cultural localization** (2 mentions across Concept B — Masahiro, Haruka) — Full Japanese-language UI, keigo handling, mixed Japanese-English code-switching support, and recognition of indirect nemawashi-style decision phrasing; Haruka called this a non-negotiable for any enterprise deployment at a Japanese company.

- **System integration & async workflow** (2 mentions across Concepts A, B — Shota, Yuki) — Automated push into SFA/CRM systems to eliminate manual copy-paste (Shota), and native Slack integration that surfaces decisions and action items in relevant channels rather than email digests, with async written-discussion coverage (Yuki).

- **Pricing structure & access model** (2 mentions across Concept B — Ayaka, Sachiko) — Solo/individual-tier pricing without minimum seat counts (Ayaka), and flat annual fee rather than creeping monthly subscription (Sachiko); both signals indicate pricing architecture is as important as the price level itself.

- **Credibility & social proof** (2 mentions across Concept B — Masahiro, Noboru) — Verified performance data from comparable Japanese companies (not startup testimonials), and documented human-review workflows that establish trust before automation is accepted in compliance-sensitive environments.
