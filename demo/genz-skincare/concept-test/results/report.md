## Preference Verdict

**Narrow lead for Concept B** (Barrier Repair Cream): 5 of 10 personas chose it (50%), placing it firmly above A (3/10, 30%) and C (2/10, 20%), but short of the threshold for a clear winner. Concept B's margin over the runner-up is 2 votes — meaningful directionally but insufficient to declare dominance with confidence. Concept C, despite the smallest share, earned the highest individual purchase likelihood scores (both choosers at 4/5), signalling outsized intensity among its narrow audience.

---

## Key Findings

1. **Barrier health as cross-segment consensus** — Concept B's lead was not driven by a single demographic but by a structurally diverse coalition: an ingredient-literate pharmacy technician (Priya), a values-driven clean beauty advocate (Jordan), a logic-first developer (Marcus), a budget-conscious teacher (Sofia), and an outdoor-active trainer (Elijah). The shared mechanism was consistent: all five framed "barrier repair" as foundational infrastructure, not cosmetic enhancement, which made it feel worth the purchase regardless of lifestyle.

2. **Concept A's appeal is need-state specific, not broadly scalable** — All three A-choosers either had an active, named skin problem (Zara's jawline breakouts), a near-zero skincare engagement level (Tyler), or were deflecting the other two concepts for gendered/cultural reasons (Derek). None was drawn to A's positive proposition; they were avoiding B and C. This reactive selection pattern depresses its growth ceiling and explains A's mean purchase likelihood of just 2.0 — the lowest of any concept's choosers.

3. **Concept C wins on emotional resonance but loses on functional credibility** — Mia and Aaliyah chose C with the panel's highest purchase intent (both at 4/5), driven by social proof (TikTok glass-skin trends, Ulta floor traffic) and aesthetic ritual integration. However, every non-chooser who mentioned C explicitly dismissed "glow boosting" as marketing language with no mechanistic basis, a phrase Jordan called the beauty industry "telling you your natural skin tone isn't enough." C's upside is real but bounded to trend-oriented, socially active younger women.

4. **Vague efficacy claims eroded confidence across all three concepts** — Concept A's "clinically proven actives" language was flagged as empty by both choosers and passers: Priya noted it was "doing a lot of heavy lifting without actually saying anything," and even Zara — who chose A — said "saying 'clinically proven' without naming the ingredients makes me nervous." Concept C's "brightens dull skin" drew parallel skepticism. Only Concept B's mechanistic framing ("strengthens your skin barrier," "restores natural protective layer") survived scrutiny, though it too was penalized for not listing specific actives.

5. **Male personas coalesced around Concept A for reasons that have little to do with A's actual merits** — Derek, Tyler, and (among female personas) none chose A for its formulation. Derek does not know what a toner is; Tyler does not know what a skin barrier is and said so explicitly. Their preference for A reflects categorical rejection of the other two concepts' positioning rather than affirmative belief in Concept A. This makes Concept A a default option for disengaged male users — a positioning that carries significant risk if that audience is ever asked to repurchase.

## Segment / Profile Splits

**Ingredient-literate / science-minded** (Priya — Ingredient Detective, Marcus — Pragmatic Upgrader, and partially Elijah — Wellness Explorer) → **Concept B** — shared rationale: mechanistic language is credible; vague claims ("glow," "clinically proven") are disqualifying without named actives and percentages.

**Values-driven / sustainability-conscious** (Jordan — Clean Beauty Advocate) → **Concept B** — barrier restoration framed as "about skin health rather than chasing an ideal," though purchase intent remained conditional on ingredient sourcing and packaging sustainability. N=1; cannot generalize across this segment.

**Trend-oriented / social-context buyers** (Aaliyah — Trend Chaser, Mia — Routine Devotee) → **Concept C** — both connected the glow toner to live cultural signals (TikTok, K-beauty ritual), with purchase intent at 4/5. Age range 20–22; both female.

**Budget-sensitive / practical** (Sofia — Budget Beauty Maven) → **Concept B** — chosen for its dupe-ability against established drugstore references (CeraVe); price threshold of ~$15 explicitly stated as a make-or-break. Derek — Reluctant Minimalist — is also price-driven but chose A and set the same ~$15 ceiling; these two personas share price sensitivity but diverge entirely on category engagement.

**Low-engagement / skeptical males** (Derek — Reluctant Minimalist, Tyler — Skincare Skeptic) → **Concept A** by elimination, not conviction. Purchase likelihood 1–2. Each is N=1; treat as directional signal about messaging barriers, not a viable target.

**Specific-need, active acne** (Zara — Anxious Perfectionist) → **Concept A** — the only chooser driven by a genuine match between stated need and concept promise. Represents the core use case Concept A was likely designed for.

## Purchase-Intent Drivers

Mechanistic specificity was the single strongest upward driver across the panel: personas who could internally verify a product's logic — barrier repair → strengthens skin's first line of defense — reported intent at 3–4, while those who encountered only aspirational language ("glow," "even complexion") without an explainable mechanism tended to stay at 3 or lower even when they chose that concept. Price transparency moved intent sharply in the opposite direction for budget-aware personas: Sofia and Derek each named a concrete ceiling (~$15) and indicated they would exit above it, suggesting undisclosed pricing suppresses conversion in the consideration phase. For Concept C's two choosers, packaging aesthetics and makeup compatibility were cited as purchase prerequisites — factors not typically included in a concept brief but which function as effective gatekeepers for an audience making shelf-selection decisions partly on camera.

## Per-Concept Strengths and Weaknesses

### Concept A (Acne Control Serum) — 3/10 chose this

**Strengths (cross-persona):** The problem-solution clarity of acne treatment provides an unambiguous reason to exist. For personas with an active, specific need — Zara most notably — the "clinically proven actives" framing implies accountability and seriousness that aspirational concepts lack. Among disengaged male personas (Derek, Tyler), A's functional directness was the least alienating option, suggesting potential for simple, no-jargon messaging in that channel.

**Weaknesses (cross-persona):** "Clinically proven actives" without disclosing which actives or at what concentration functioned as a credibility liability rather than an asset; it was the most-criticized phrase on the panel, flagged by Priya, Zara, and Mia. Purchase likelihood among A-choosers averaged just 2.0 — the lowest of any concept's supporters — driven by Derek's and Tyler's near-zero category engagement. The concept also lacks a retention hook: acne treatment is inherently episodic, and two of three choosers (Derek, Tyler) framed it as a reluctant, one-time fix rather than a routine product.

---

### Concept B (Barrier Repair Cream) — 5/10 chose this

**Strengths (cross-persona):** Barrier repair's positioning as foundational skin health — rather than cosmetic enhancement — gave it purchase across otherwise incompatible worldviews. Elijah framed it through athletic recovery logic; Marcus through engineering efficiency; Sofia through category familiarity with CeraVe; Jordan through anti-aesthetics values. This conceptual flexibility is a meaningful strategic asset. It was also the only concept whose core claim survived ingredient-literate scrutiny without being labeled "fluff."

**Weaknesses (cross-persona):** Concept B's label as "barrier repair" actively repelled the trend and social segments: Aaliyah dismissed it as giving "dry skin doctor visit, not selfie ready," and Mia noted it doesn't fit a glass-skin aesthetic narrative. Despite leading in preference, purchase likelihood for B-choosers averaged 3.2 — respectable but not enthused — held back universally by the absence of disclosed actives. Every single B-chooser named ingredient transparency as a purchase prerequisite.

---

### Concept C (Glow Boosting Toner) — 2/10 chose this

**Strengths (cross-persona):** Among its two choosers, C earned the panel's peak purchase intent scores (4/5 each), and both cited real-world social proof that is difficult to manufacture: Aaliyah observes customer demand at Ulta daily; Mia works in clean beauty and tracks trend curves. The toner format's fit with established K/J-beauty multi-step routines is a structural advantage for ritual-oriented users, not just a trend coincidence.

**Weaknesses (cross-persona):** "Glow boosting" and "more even complexion" were perceived as unverifiable marketing language by the majority of the panel — including non-choosers Priya, Marcus, Zara, and Sofia — who each independently reached the same verdict: it says nothing mechanistic. Jordan raised the additional concern that "even complexion" framing implicitly pathologizes natural skin tone variation, a reading that may limit appeal in DEI-conscious audiences. C's market is real but demographically narrow (young, female, social-media-active) and conceptually fragile if the "glass skin" trend cycle turns.

## Improvement Theme Clusters

- **Disclose specific actives and concentrations** (8 mentions across A, B, C) — The panel's most universal request, spanning every concept and nearly every segment: name the ingredients (ceramides, niacinamide, alpha-arbutin, azelaic acid, salicylic acid) and publish their percentages. Priya, Marcus, Elijah, and Sofia each stated they would not purchase without cross-referencing a formulation; Zara said undisclosed actives "makes me nervous." This is not a niche ask — it is a baseline credibility requirement for an ingredient-literate generation.

- **Provide price transparency upfront** (2 mentions, Concepts A and B — Derek, Sofia) — Both Derek and Sofia named explicit price ceilings ($15 and drugstore-equivalence respectively) and framed undisclosed pricing as a potential exit trigger. Sofia specified she would "find the drugstore equivalent" if a barrier product exceeds $30, signalling that accessible price anchoring could pre-empt competitive substitution.

- **Improve packaging aesthetics and shelf/social presence** (2 mentions, Concept C — Aaliyah, Jordan) — Aaliyah requires packaging that reads well on TikTok backgrounds; Jordan wants to eliminate single-use plastic. Though coming from opposite motivations, both represent a segment that treats packaging as a functional purchase criterion, not a superficial one.

- **Add makeup-compatibility claim** (1 mention, Concept C — Aaliyah) — Specifically requested for the toner: confirmation that the product layers under foundation without pilling or affecting wear. N=1 but represents a high-frequency use case for the concept's core audience.

- **Reframe or rename for low-engagement male audiences** (2 mentions, Concept A — Tyler, Derek) — Tyler asked to "call it something that doesn't sound like a skincare commercial"; Derek did not know what a toner was. Neither will be converted by ingredient education alone; format and language register are the barrier, not formulation.

- **Disclose sustainability and ethical sourcing credentials** (1 mention, Concept B — Jordan) — Cruelty-free status, packaging material, and ingredient provenance were cited as preconditions for purchase. N=1 in this panel, but Clean Beauty Advocate is a segment with growing market share; the absence of any sustainability signal was read as a gap, not a neutral.

## Caveats

- Virtual panel of 10 personas; directional only — each segment represented by exactly N=1 persona; segment-level conclusions should not be over-generalized
- Not statistically representative; use for hypothesis generation and concept refinement, not go/no-go decisions
- AI-generated responses may exhibit positivity bias; purchase likelihood scores of 3–4 should be interpreted conservatively
